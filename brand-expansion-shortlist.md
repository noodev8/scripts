## How to run the margin gate (the delegate does this — judgement per style, per channel)

We never own the listing. Every Amazon listing is crowded, IVES included — what wins is **pricing well + holding stock availability when rivals run dry**, while still clearing loaded cost. So the gate is not "is the ASIN ours" — it's **"is there a channel and a price where this clears loaded cost?"**

A brand that loses on Amazon has not failed — it has failed *one configuration*. The fees and the price you're pinned to differ by channel, so run both before dropping anything. This is the ECCO trap: we ran only the Amazon config — high referral + FBA, pinned to Amazon's floored buy-box price — declared a fail, and stopped. Drop the referral, drop the FBA, set our own Shopify price, and the same brand can clear.

**A. Loaded cost — compute it twice, the stacks differ.**
Start from one buy cost (a *range*, 40–50% of observed RRP — don't trust a single point), then load it two ways:
- **Amazon-loaded:** buy + VAT + Amazon referral (~15%) + FBA + DPD + wages + storage. (Same stack that takes IVES from raw ~£14 to ~£5.80 net.)
- **Shopify-loaded:** buy + VAT + our own fulfilment + DPD + wages + storage — **no referral, no FBA.** Materially lower, and that's the whole point: a brand Amazon races to the floor can still clear on Shopify.
- Report against the **pessimistic (50%) buy cost** — "works even if we negotiate badly" vs "only works with hard terms" is the useful distinction.

**B. Live street price + how contested (read off the actual listing, not RRP).**
For each hero style open the live Amazon UK listing and read:
- current **buy-box price** (the real Amazon trading number);
- **seller count** + who they are (brand/Amazon-direct vs grey EU arbitrage) — i.e. how hard the buy box is to hold;
- whether the **brand is discounting it themselves** (the Tamaris / Marco Tozzi / Jana trap).
- On Shopify we are **not** pinned to the buy-box — we set our own price. So also ask: at a price that clears Shopify-loaded cost, are we competitive with the *wider* market (not just Amazon)?

**C. Verdict per style — "where does this work?", not pass/fail.**

| Brand · Style | RRP | est. buy (40–50%) | Amazon-loaded | Shopify-loaded | live Amazon buy-box | Amazon margin | Shopify margin (our price) | sellers / contested? | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Verdict is one of:
- **Amazon-core** — clears Amazon-loaded at a buy-box we can hold on price + stock.
- **Shopify-core** — fails or is floor-raced on Amazon, but clears Shopify-loaded at a price we set ourselves (the ECCO case: don't list on Amazon at all).
- **Both** — clears both; lead with the stronger search channel.
- **Drop** — can't clear loaded cost on *either* channel's best realistic configuration, or the price is competed below breakeven everywhere regardless of execution.

Only **Drop** stops the brand — and only after the Shopify column is run, never on an Amazon fail alone.
- **Margin floor (judgement, not a cutoff):** ~£2 net/unit is the rough minimum to bother. But weigh it as *return on capital, not £ per unit* — £2 on a £40 buy is a worse business than £2 on a £16 buy (IVES clears ~£5.80 on sub-£20 cost and turns fast; that's the bar). The cheaper and faster-turning the stock, the lower the absolute £ you'll accept. This is the call the delegate is here to make — the table gives them the numbers to make it against.

### The comparison that matters — return on capital, not £/unit

**Measure every candidate against these two benchmarks, not against its own RRP or any specific price.** The prices below are incidental; the *ratio of profit to cash tied up* is the whole point.

| Benchmark | Sale | Cost (cash tied up) | Net/unit | ROC per turn | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **IVES** | ~£39 | ~£16–20 | **~£5** | **~36%** | **The bar** — best deployment of capital we have |
| **ECCO @ £80** | £80 | £45 | **~£5** | **~11%** | **The anti-pattern** — *same ~£5 profit, but on 2.5× the cash* |

**Both make roughly £5/unit — and that is exactly why £/unit is the wrong lens.** ECCO ties up £45 to earn the same fiver IVES earns on under £20. Same result, far more cash locked up per turn → a much worse business. So the screen is: **for the same profit, which candidate frees up the most capital to turn again?**

**Why this is the point of the whole exercise:** IVES is the best thing we own, but we can't put unlimited capital into one product (stock depth, demand ceiling, supplier limits). **Once IVES is stocked to where it should be, the next pound of capital has to go somewhere** — and the right question is *not* "is it as good as IVES?" (almost nothing will be) but **"is it the best available home for the capital we can't put into more IVES?"** A candidate ranks below IVES and still earns its place — provided its ROC beats the alternatives (e.g. ECCO's ~11%, or sitting on cash). Marco Tozzi @ 40% (~£4.50 on £18 → ~25% ROC) is the live example: below IVES, well above ECCO — a plausible second home for capital.

**Then, survivors → step 2 (search) → confirm the channel call.**

**D. Shopify-core has a hidden gate — customer-acquisition cost.**
The loaded-cost stacks above are not symmetric in *how the buyer arrives*. On Amazon the marketplace hands you the buyer for the price of the referral fee (already in the Amazon stack). On Shopify there is no free traffic — we buy it with Google Ads / SEO, and that cost is **not** in the Shopify-loaded number. IVES "clears Shopify-loaded" partly because it rides existing store traffic; a cold new brand with no search pull means we'd be *paying* to acquire every sale on top of the loaded cost.

So a **Shopify-core** verdict is not "clears Shopify-loaded cost" — it's **"clears Shopify-loaded cost *plus* realistic customer-acquisition cost, given how searched the brand/style is."** For Shopify-core, step 2 (search demand) is therefore **load-bearing, not a tiebreaker**: a brand nobody searches by name has no cheap-traffic path, so "Shopify-core" collapses into "drop" no matter how good the per-unit loaded margin looks. Branded search volume (people typing the brand/style name) is the proxy for cheap traffic — the higher it is, the more the Shopify-loaded margin is real money rather than ad spend.

> **Reality check (2026-06-16) — Shopify-core is mostly a mirage for no-moat brands.** Our only genuine *Shopify* winner is **Birkenstock (240K UK branded searches/mo) — a moat/monopoly**, not a "we priced it well" play. **Rieker (9.9K branded/mo), which we own, is *still* "more of an Amazon product"** and only ticks along on Shopify (~10% margin). The lesson: **~10K branded searches did not build a good Shopify brand** — so 1–2K never will. The store's audience is Birkenstock-led and does not convert mid-market comfort brands well *regardless* of how clean the Shopify-loaded math looks. **Do not treat the ECCO "Amazon-fail → Shopify rescue" as a reliable second life.** It is a rare exception (real moat, or freakishly strong + convertible demand), not the default. Treat a Shopify-core verdict with suspicion unless there's a moat or demand far above the Rieker bar.

> **Footnote — moat:** there's no moat on these yet, and that shouldn't stop the investigation. But if a brand turns out Amazon-restricted (Shopify runs clean) or hands us an ownable listing, flag it — that's the upgrade from "works" to "winner." A question to explore, not a gate.
