# Online Front Door — Business Plan

## What It Is

A managed online presence service for small local businesses. We build it, they update it themselves from their phone. No tech skills, no agency, no WordPress.

**Parent company:** Noodev8 (noodev8.com)
**Product brand:** Online Front Door (onlinefrontdoor.co.uk / .com)
**Customer platform:** ofd.net (e.g. nagshead.ofd.net)

## The Market

Millions of small businesses have no online presence, or are stuck paying an agency for every small change. Our customers are non-technical local business owners who want to be found online and keep their info up to date without depending on anyone.

**Target sectors:** pubs, cafes, coffee shops, barbers, salons, takeaways, trades (plumbers, electricians, builders), small gyms, local shops.

**We are not competing with website builders.** We're competing with having nothing, or being trapped with an agency. The customer who would happily use Wix is not our customer. The one who wouldn't touch Wix is.

## The Vision

Start as a simple managed service. Grow into a platform — the Shopify for small local businesses. A single system that powers hundreds or thousands of sites, each independently managed by the business owner. Over time, add features the market asks for: online ordering, pre-orders, booking, events, loyalty. The portfolio grows, recurring revenue compounds, and most customers need zero ongoing work once settled.

## Phases

### Phase 1 — Platform Build (Now)
Get site creation from 2 days down towards under an hour. Generalise the template to handle different business types via configuration (pub needs menus, barber needs services/pricing, plumber needs areas covered). Build a simple admin flow so creating a new site is data entry, not development.

**Staff member can join here** — learning the codebase, building sites, helping systematise the process.

### Phase 2 — Manual Sales
Get the first 10-20 customers across different sectors. Build prototypes using publicly available info, drop leaflets, follow the nurture process. Prove the conversion works for barbers and plumbers, not just pubs.

**Key question to answer:** which business types convert best? Focus there first.

### Phase 3 — Commission Reps
Once the conversion process is proven and repeatable:
- Document the process so a rep can follow it
- Recruit 1-2 reps on straight commission (50% of year 1)
- Give them a territory and a batch of preview sites to work with
- You build the sites, they do the legwork

### Phase 4 — Paid Acquisition
Once the funnel is proven through reps:
- Local Facebook/Google ads
- Mailshots to targeted business lists
- Scale the messaging that's already been tested manually

### Phase 5 — Platform Features
Driven by what customers actually ask for. Potential features:
- **Online ordering / pre-orders** (restaurants, cafes, takeaways)
- **Booking system** (barbers, salons, gyms)
- **Events / what's on** (pubs, venues)
- **Reviews integration**
- **Google Business Profile sync**
- **Email/SMS to their customers**

Each feature is a potential price tier increase. Build only what's requested — don't speculate.

## Growth Model

The portfolio compounds:
1. New customers add to the recurring base
2. Most customers settle quickly and need no ongoing work
3. A few need more support initially, but that tapers
4. Revenue grows every month without losing previous months
5. Features unlock higher pricing tiers over time
6. More customers = more examples = easier sales = more customers

This is the Brookfield model applied to software: build the system, let it run, add to it, compound.

### Churn — The Number You Must Watch

SMB SaaS typically sees 15-25% annual churn. This is the most important metric in the business.

| Annual churn rate | Lost customers/year at 5,000 | New customers needed/month just to replace |
|---|---|---|
| 10% (excellent) | 500 | ~42 |
| 15% (good) | 750 | ~63 |
| 20% (average) | 1,000 | ~83 |
| 25% (concerning) | 1,250 | ~104 |

At 20% churn with 5,000 customers, you need 83 new customers per month to stand still, plus more to grow. That's why the sales engine matters as much as the product.

**Churn reduction levers:**
- Low price = low motivation to cancel (£50/month isn't worth the hassle of deciding)
- Genuine utility — they need their hours/info online, it's not a nice-to-have once they're using it
- Switching cost — their URL is on their signage, cards, Google listing. Moving away is effort
- Feature additions over time increase stickiness (booking, ordering, events)
- Annual payment option at a discount (e.g. £500/year = save £100) locks in commitment

### What Has to Be True for 5,000 to Work

1. **The sales engine produces consistently.** 50-100 new customers per month at scale. This is the hardest part — not the tech, not the product. Commission reps + paid acquisition + mailshots all contributing.

2. **Churn stays under 20%.** Low price and genuine utility help. Annual payment option helps. Features that make the site more embedded in their business help.

3. **Site creation stays efficient.** At 100 new sites per month, if each takes 2 days you need 7-8 full-time site builders. If each takes 1 hour, you need 1-2. Platform investment pays for itself here.

4. **Support doesn't scale linearly.** 90% of sites should be zero-maintenance once settled. If support scales with customers 1:1, the model breaks. Self-service has to work.

5. **You build a management layer.** At 15+ people you're running a company, not a project. Departments, processes, reporting. This is a different skill from building systems — but you've done it at Brookfield with staff and premises.

### The Shopify Analogy

Shopify started as a simple tool for small shops to sell online. They didn't compete with enterprise e-commerce. They owned the long tail — millions of small businesses who needed something simple.

OFD is the same play for online presence. Not competing with agencies or WordPress. Owning the long tail of local businesses who need something simple, managed, and affordable. The platform grows features over time, ARPU increases, and the customer base compounds.
